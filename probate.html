Administration of an Estate in Las Vegas

Estate administration is often a slow process that involves looking at a decedent's assets and dividing them according to law and his or her wishes. General administration involves categorizing assets not listed in a trust and then handing them down according to the decedent's will (or intestate statutes if there is no will). Normally, a close family member is appointed to handle a probate case unless the decedent has appointed a specific person as executor in his or her will.

Supervised or unsupervised?

When arranging an estate for probate, parties need to choose whether they are going to opt for supervised or unsupervised administration. Supervised administration will involve the court.

We suggest that you get the court involved if:

  • There are disputes about the will's validity
  • The will contains vague, disputable language
  • Beneficiaries disagree on the estate's administration
  • The decedent died intestate, and the heirs are not known
  • The estate contains assets that are difficult to value or sell

Unsupervised administration is permissible if the estate is solvent, which means that it has more assets than debts, and if the will authorizes it. Also, all heirs to the will must agree for unsupervised administration. A court-appointed executor will be required to handle the administration and sale of all assets and will not be required to request court approval for decisions.

What is special administration?

In special administration, a person is appointed by the court to take charge of a designated portion of the state during probate. For example, the special administrator that has expertise on antiques may be appointed to work with all furniture and antiques in an estate. A special administrator can also be a person who is appointed to be responsible for the deceased person's property for a limited amount of time or in the case of an emergency. Normally, special administrators are required to maintain or preserve an estate, not just to take control of the probate process.

Summary Administration Can Be Quicker

Summary administration is a probate shortcut that is used in Nevada for estates that have a value of less than $200,000. It is only an option if the value of the estate is less than $200,000.

To complete this faster form of probate, the nominated executor of a will or an administrator of an intestate estate must file a document called the petition for summary administration. Any beneficiary of the estate must be served notice that the petition was filed. The petition will state that the estate qualifies for summary administration and list the deceased person's assets and their values. If the estate is approved for summary administration, then the court will issue an order allowing the probate to proceed as a summary administration, which will shorten the time required to complete the probate before distributing the estate assets to the decedent's heirs or beneficiaries.

Probate & Smaller Estates

If you are working with a smaller estate, Nevada offers several helpful options. One option you may want to explore a "set aside" petition. If you are approved to set aside the estate, it will go through a simplified procedure transferring all assets to the surviving spouse, minor child, or heirs of the decedent. A set aside is available if the estate does not exceed $100,000.00. A set aside may protect the decedent's spouse or minor children against claims of the decedent's creditors.

Set-aside petitions are valid if your estate meets the following requirements:

  • The request is filed in the form of a petition to set aside estate.
  • The request is filed by a decedent's surviving spouse, minor child, heir, or executor.
  • The value of the decedent's estate does not exceed $100,000 at the date of death

Individuals may also be able to file an affidavit of entitlement. This is another way to simplify the probate process for estates valued at less than $20,000. Estates only qualify for this simple process if they don't include any interest in real property, including a mortgage, trust deed, or lien. Anyone can file for an affidavit of entitlement as long as he or she is entitled to the assets of the estate.
